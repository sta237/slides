Recap: Law of large numbers

Law of large numbers

Suppose \(X_1\), \(X_2\), …, \(X_n\) are independent random variables with expectation \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\). Then for any \(\varepsilon > 0\),

\[\lim_{n\to\infty}P\left(\left|\overline{X}_n-\mu\right|>\varepsilon\right)=0,\]

where \(\overline{X}_n=\left.\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right/n\).

  • Convergence in probability suggest that the sampling distribution of \(\overline{X}_n\) becomes narrower as \(n\) increases
  • The convergence occurs regardless of the originating distribution

  • We also observe that their distributions become roughly symmetric bell shapes with a larger sample size
  • This behaviour also seems to occur regardless of the underlying distribution shape

Convergence in distribution

Let \(Y_1\), \(Y_2\), \(Y_3\), … be an infinite sequence of random variables, and let \(W\) be another random variable. Then, we say the sequence \(\left\{Y_n\right\}\) converges in distribution to \(W\) if for all \(w\in\mathbb{R}\) such that \(P\left(W = w\right)=0\), we have

\[\lim_{n\to\infty}P\left(Y_n\le w\right)=P\left(W\le w\right)\]

and we write

\[Y_n\overset{d}{\to}W.\]

  • The distribution of \(Y_n\) becomes closer and closer to that of \(W\)

Example: 4.4.2 from Evans & Rosenthal

Suppose \(X_n\) is a sequency of random variables with

\[P\left(X_n=x\right) = \begin{cases}\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{n} & \text{when } x= 0 \\ \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{n} & \text{when } x= 1\end{cases}\]

  • As \(n\to\infty\), \(P(X_n=x)\to\frac{1}{2}\) for both \(x=0\) and \(x=1\)
  • \(X_n\overset{d}{\to}X\) where \(X\sim\text{Ber}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)\)

Example: Binomial for inifinite trials

Suppose \(X_n\sim\text{Binom}\left(n,p\right)\) describes the number of successes out of \(n\) independent sub-interval of equal-lengths.

What happens when you make the sub-intervals smaller and smaller to infinitesimally small?

That is, what happens when you take \(n\) to infinity?

  • First let’s define the overall success rate as \(\lambda = np\) and write the pmf in terms of \(\lambda\) and \(p\)
  • \(P\left(X_n=x\right)=\binom{n}{x}\left(\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^x\left(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{n-x}=\frac{n!}{x!\left(n-x\right)!}\left(\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^x\left(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{n-x}\)
  • We then take \(n\) to infinity
  • \(\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n!}{x!\left(n-x\right)!}\left(\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^x\left(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{n-x}=\frac{\lambda^x}{x!}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n!}{\left(n-x\right)!n^x}\cdot \left(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^n\)
  • \(=\frac{\lambda^x}{x!}\cdot 1\cdot e^{-\lambda}=\frac{\lambda^x e^{-\lambda}}{x!}\)

\[X_n\overset{d}{\to}X\quad\text{where}\quad X\sim\text{Pois}\left(\lambda\right)\]

The central limit theorem

Let \(X_1\), \(X_2\), \(X_3\), … be independent and identically distributed random variables with finite expectation \(\mu\) and finite positive variance \(\sigma^2\). For \(n\ge1\), let

\[Z_n=\frac{\sqrt{n}\left(\overline{X}_n-\mu\right)}{\sigma},\]

where \(\overline{X}_n=\left.\sum_{i=1}^nX_i\right/n\).

Then for any number \(a\in\mathbb{R}\),

\[\lim_{n\to\infty}P\left(Z_n\le a\right)=\Phi\left(a\right),\]

where \(\Phi\) is the cumulative distribution function of the standard normal distribution. In other words,

\[\frac{\sqrt{n}\left(\overline{X_n}-\mu\right)}{\sigma}\overset{d}{\to}Z,\]

where \(Z\sim N\left(0,1\right)\).

Equivalently …

\[\frac{\sqrt{n}\left(\overline{X_n}-\mu\right)}{\sigma}\overset{d}{\to}Z\]

\[\overline{X}_n\overset{d}{\to}\left(Z\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}+\mu\right)\]

\[\lim_{n\to\infty}P\left(\overline{X}_n\le a\right)=\Phi\left(\frac{a-\mu}{\left.\sigma\right/\sqrt{n}}\right)\]

Example: Canadian Income Survey (CIS)

Statistics Canada conducts the CIS every year. Suppose 1,000 households are randomly selected from Toronto for the survey in 2022. Assume that random variable \(X\) describes the income distribution among households in Toronto in 2022 with pdf \(f\).

\[f(x)=\frac{9}{x^{10}}, x\ge 1\]

What is the probability that the average household income amount the survey participants is more than 1/40 units away from the population mean \(E(X)\)?

Let \(X_1\), \(X_2\), …, \(X_{1000}\) be the household income amount of the survey participants. We are interested in \(P\left(\left|\overline{X}_{1000} - E\left[X\right]\right| > 1/40 \right)\). Assume \(X_i\) are independent.

  • Computing the exact \(F_{\overline{X}_{1000}}\) using the sum of independent continuous random variables would be very difficult and time consuming

  • We can instead use the CLT to approximate the distribution of \(\overline{X}_{1000}\)

  • \(P\left(\left|\overline{X}_{1000} - E\left[X\right]\right| > \frac{1}{40}\right)\)
    \(= P\left(\overline{X}_{1000}>E(X) +\frac{1}{40}\right) + P\left(\overline{X}_{1000} < E(X) -\frac{1}{40}\right)\)
  • \(1 - P\left(\overline{X}_{1000}<E(X) +\frac{1}{40}\right) + P\left(\overline{X}_{1000}<E(X) -\frac{1}{40}\right)\)
  • \(\approx 1-\Phi\left(\frac{1/40}{\sigma/\sqrt{1000}}\right) + \Phi\left(\frac{-1/40}{\sigma/\sqrt{1000}}\right)\)
  • \(=0.063\)

Example: Normal approximation of the binomial distribution

Suppose \(Y\sim \text{Binom}(50, 0.3)\) and we are interested in \(P(Y\le 20)\).

  • We can compute the probability by summing up \(p_Y(y)\) for \(y=0,1,2...,20\)
  • Or, we can use the CLT approximate the probability since \(Y\) is sum of 50 independent Bernoulli random variables, say, \(W_1\), \(W_2\), …, \(W_{50}\)

\[P(Y\le 20) = P\left(\sum_{i=1}^{50}W_i\le 20\right)=P\left(\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{50} W_i}{50} \le \frac{20}{50}\right)=P\left(\overline{W}_{50}\le 0.4\right)\]

\[E\left[\overline{W}_{50}\right]=0.3\]

\[\text{Var}\left(\overline{W}_{50}\right)=\frac{0.3\cdot0.7}{50}=\frac{0.21}{50}\]

\[\implies P(Y\le 20) \approx \Phi\left(\frac{0.4-0.3}{\sqrt{0.21/50}}\right)=0.9386\]

The exact probability is pbinom(20, 50, 0.3)=0.9522.

We used pnorm(0.4, 0.3, sqrt(0.21 / 50)) to estimate the probability.
